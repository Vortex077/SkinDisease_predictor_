What is it?

Melanoma is a type of skin cancer that originates in the pigment-producing cells called melanocytes. Melanocytes are responsible for producing melanin, the pigment that gives color to the skin, hair, and eyes.
Melanoma can develop when these cells undergo malignant transformations and start to grow uncontrollably.

Risk factors for melanoma include:

1. Exposure to Ultraviolet (UV) Radiation: Overexposure to UV radiation from the sun or tanning beds is a significant risk factor.

2. Fair Skin: People with fair skin, light hair, and light-colored eyes are at a higher risk.

3. Family History: If close relatives have had melanoma, it may increase your risk.

4. Weakened Immune System: Individuals with a weakened immune system may have an increased risk.

5. Numerous Moles: Having a large number of moles or unusual moles may be a risk factor.

What are the symptoms?

The symptoms of Melanoma can vary, and it's important to be vigilant about any changes in your skin. Here are some common signs and symptoms of Melanoma:

1. Changes in Existing Moles:

Asymmetry: One half of the mole does not match the other half.
Border irregularity: The edges of the mole are uneven or jagged.
Color: Uneven color or multiple colors within the same mole.
Diameter: The mole is larger than the size of a pencil eraser.
Evolution: Changes in size, shape, color, or elevation over time.

2. New Moles or Growth: The appearance of a new, unusual mole or growth on the skin.

3. Itching or Tenderness: Some melanomas may cause itching, tenderness, or pain.

4. Bleeding or Oozing: Melanomas can bleed or ooze, and the surface may become crusty.

5. Darkening of the Skin: The skin around a mole may become darker.

6. Elevation or Bump: The mole may become raised or develop a bump.

It's important to note that not all changes in moles or skin growths indicate melanoma, but any suspicious changes should be examined by a healthcare professional, preferably a dermatologist. Early detection is crucial for successful treatment of melanoma.
